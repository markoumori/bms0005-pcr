PCR Basics & Theory

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) is a revolutionary technique used to amplify a specific segment of DNA, generating millions of copies from a tiny initial sample.

1. The Master Mix (The Recipe)

A successful PCR reaction requires five essential components mixed together in a small tube.

Template DNA

The original sample containing the target sequence you want to copy.

Primers (Fwd & Rev)

Short, single-stranded DNA sequences that flank the target region, acting as start signals.

Taq Polymerase

A heat-stable enzyme responsible for synthesizing the new DNA strands.

dNTPs

The free nucleotide building blocks (A, C, G, T) used to build the new DNA.

Buffer & MgCl₂

Provides the optimal chemical environment and necessary cofactors for the enzyme to function.

2. The Thermal Cycle

The tube is placed in a thermocycler, which rapidly changes temperatures to drive the reaction. This 3-step cycle is repeated 25-35 times.

94-98°C

Step 1: Denaturation

The high heat breaks the hydrogen bonds holding the double-stranded DNA together, resulting in two single strands.

50-65°C

Step 2: Annealing

The temperature is lowered to allow the Forward and Reverse primers to bind (anneal) to their complementary sequences on the single-stranded template. The exact temperature depends on the primers' Melting Temperature (Tm).

72°C

Step 3: Extension

The temperature is raised to the optimal working temperature for Taq Polymerase. The enzyme binds to the primers and adds dNTPs to build the new complementary DNA strands.

3. Concept Verification

Click the questions below to verify your understanding.

Heat Stability. Human DNA polymerase denatures (destroys) at the extreme temperatures required for step one. Taq polymerase was isolated from a thermophilic bacterium (Thermus aquaticus) found in hot springs, so it easily survives the 95°C denaturation step.

Non-specific binding. If the temperature is too low, the primers might bind to random, slightly mismatched sequences across the entire genome, leading to the amplification of unwanted, incorrect DNA products.

Gel Electrophoresis. PCR happens in a clear liquid; you can't see the result with the naked eye. We load the product into an agarose gel, run an electrical current through it to separate the DNA fragments by size, and use a fluorescent dye to see if a band appeared at our expected size.

Primers binding to each other. If the forward and reverse primers have complementary sequences (especially at their 3' ends), they will bind to each other instead of the template DNA. Taq polymerase will then extend them, creating a short, unwanted "primer-dimer" product that wastes reaction reagents.
